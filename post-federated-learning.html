Building a Federated Learning System for ICU Sepsis Prediction: Lessons from MIMIC-III

Sepsis kills up to 20% of ICU patients globally, yet treatment protocols remain one-size-fits-all. I built a safe offline RL system with hierarchical and multimodal architectures — trained on MIMIC-III without ever centralising patient records.

The Clinical AI Privacy Problem

Clinical AI has a paradox at its centre. To build reliable models for rare but lethal conditions like sepsis, you need patient data from many hospitals — diverse populations, different treatment protocols, different equipment. But hospital data cannot be shared. HIPAA, GDPR, and institutional ethics boards all prohibit it, and rightly so. Patient records are among the most sensitive data that exists.

Federated learning is the principled solution to this paradox. Instead of centralising data, you distribute the model. Each hospital trains on its own local data and shares only the updated model parameters — never the underlying records. A central server aggregates these updates into a global model that benefits from all sites' learning without any patient ever leaving a hospital's firewall.

This project implements exactly that architecture, using the MIMIC-III clinical database as the ground truth dataset, and building two complementary pipelines that together form a comprehensive clinical intelligence platform — from interpretable supervised baselines through to privacy-preserving foundation models.

About MIMIC-III: The Medical Information Mart for Intensive Care III is a freely accessible critical care database from Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center (2001–2012). It contains data from 60,000+ ICU patients across 26 related tables and 39 million timestamped events. Access requires PhysioNet credentialed registration.

Two-Pipeline Architecture

The repository is structured as two independent pipelines that build on each other. Pipeline 1 establishes supervised baselines and validates the clinical hypotheses. Pipeline 2 takes those validated models into a privacy-preserving, federated context with continuous-time dynamics. Reading them in sequence is the clearest way to understand why each architectural decision was made.

Pipeline 1: Integrated Clinical Assistant

This is the foundational pipeline — a classic supervised ML approach that establishes baselines and validates data quality before introducing more complex architectures. It combines three distinct AI techniques into a single, cohesive clinical assistant that serves as the operational centrepiece of the project:

Component Core Technique Inputs & Data Sources Clinical Rationale & Target
01 — Sepsis Survival Prediction Random Forest Classifier Vital sign aggregates (HR, SpO₂), lab summaries (lactate, creatinine), unit type, and demographics. Achieves baseline 0.75+ AUC. Provides interpretable feature importance rankings vital for clinical trust.
02 — Risk Factor Discovery Graph Neural Network (GNN) Patient comorbidity network built from DIAGNOSES_ICD (diagnoses as nodes, co-occurrence as edges). Learns node embeddings that cluster comorbidity burdens, making complex structural risk factors highly visible.
03 — Treatment Optimisation Q-Learning (Offline RL) Treatment-outcome tuples from PROCEDURES_ICD (discretised fluids, vasopressors, ventilation). Converges over 1,000+ episodes to recommend optimal treatment trajectories mapped to survival.
04 — Integrated Assistant MIMICClinicalAssistant Unified interface wrapper accepting real-time patient physiological vector. Outputs tri-component predictions: survival probability (ML), comorbidity risks (GNN), and suggested trajectory (RL).

Pipeline 2: Federated Foundation Architecture

This is the research-grade pipeline. It addresses the core limitation of Pipeline 1: centralised training data. All of Pipeline 1's models require all patient records to be in one place. Pipeline 2 removes that requirement entirely — making the approach deployable across real hospital networks where data localisation is mandatory.

GNN for Disease Co-occurrence

The comorbidity graph is one of the most clinically interesting components of this project. Tabular analysis tells you what features correlate with sepsis mortality. Graph analysis tells you which diseases co-occur with sepsis at anomalous rates — revealing the structural disease burden of a critical care cohort.

Construction is straightforward: iterate the DIAGNOSES_ICD table, build an adjacency matrix where A[i][j] = number of patients with both condition i and condition j. Normalise by individual diagnosis prevalence to get a co-occurrence excess score. Apply a GNN with message-passing layers to learn embeddings that encode the local graph neighbourhood of each diagnosis — conditions with similar co-occurrence profiles cluster together in embedding space.

The resulting comorbidity heatmaps reveal clinically expected clusters (sepsis, acute kidney injury, multi-organ failure) but also surfaced less obvious associations worth further investigation — a validation that the graph approach can generate genuinely novel clinical hypotheses from historical data.

Continuous-Time Dynamics: ACSSM

ICU data is inherently irregular. Vital signs are measured when a nurse checks them. Lab results arrive when the lab processes a sample. Standard RNNs and Transformers assume fixed-interval inputs — a fundamentally wrong assumption for clinical time-series data that will produce systematic prediction errors wherever measurement timing is driven by patient deterioration (which is exactly when you need the model most).

The Attentive Continuous State-Space Model (ACSSM) addresses this directly. Rather than treating patient observations as a discrete sequence, it models the patient's physiological state as a continuous latent trajectory governed by a learned ordinary differential equation. Between observations, the hidden state evolves according to this ODE. When a new observation arrives, the model updates its latent estimate using a learned attention mechanism that weights the new measurement against the predicted state.

The ACSSM avoids the interpolation artifacts that plague naive approaches like linear imputation. It learns how physiological variables evolve between measurements — capturing the fact that heart rate recovers differently from blood pressure, which recovers differently from lactate. This dynamic is clinically meaningful: the rate of change of a vital sign is often more informative than its absolute value.

Temporal Attention Mechanisms

Once the ACSSM has produced a continuous latent trajectory, the temporal attention module identifies which historical states are most relevant for the current prediction. The core insight is that different physiological variables decay in relevance at different rates — a heart rate reading from 2 minutes ago is still highly relevant; a lactate measurement from 6 hours ago is not. The module implements three decay functions that can be selected based on the clinical variable being modelled:

Decay TypeFormulaBest ForClinical Rationale
Exponentialexp(-λΔt)Fast-changing vitals (HR, SpO₂)Continuous monitoring; recent values dominate
Gaussianexp(-Δt²/2σ²)Lab values with known test intervalsPeaked relevance around expected re-measurement time
Power-law(1+Δt)⁻ᵅLong-range dependencies (chronic markers)Chronic conditions retain relevance over days

The multi-scale design allows the model to simultaneously attend to short-term physiological crises (deteriorating lactate in the last 2 hours) and longer-term context (the patient's admission trajectory over 48 hours). This is architecturally analogous to multi-scale attention in NLP transformers, but grounded in the physiology of critical care rather than token co-occurrence statistics.

The Federated Learning Framework

The federated setup simulates multiple hospital nodes, each training on a partitioned subset of MIMIC-III data, then aggregating updates via a central server. Privacy is enforced at two independent levels — model architecture and training algorithm — so that no single point of failure can expose patient data.

Differential Privacy (DP-SGD)

During local training, gradient noise is added at each update step before parameters are shared. The privacy budget ε bounds the maximum information about any single patient's record that can be inferred from the released model weights — even by an adversary with access to all other data. A tighter budget (smaller ε) provides stronger privacy guarantees but at the cost of model accuracy. The implementation uses the Opacus library, which integrates directly with PyTorch's optimiser loop:

Federated_Foundation_Models/src/federated_foundation_models.ipynb Python 
from opacus import PrivacyEngine

# Attach DP-SGD to the local training loop
privacy_engine = PrivacyEngine()
model, optimiser, train_loader = privacy_engine.make_private_with_epsilon(
    module=local_foundation_model,
    optimizer=optimiser,
    data_loader=train_loader,
    epochs=num_epochs,
    target_epsilon=3.0,   # privacy budget ε — lower = stronger privacy
    target_delta=1e-5,    # probability of ε-violation (δ)
    max_grad_norm=1.0,    # gradient clipping bound before noise addition
)

# Opacus automatically computes noise scale σ from (ε, δ, epochs)
# and clips + perturbs each per-sample gradient in the batch

Secure Aggregation (FedAvg)

Model updates from each simulated hospital are aggregated at the central server using weighted FedAvg — the canonical federated aggregation algorithm. The weight for each site is proportional to its number of local training samples, ensuring that larger hospitals contribute more to the global model while smaller sites still benefit from the collective knowledge. Crucially, the server never receives raw patient data — only the weight deltas from the local training step.

def federated_average(local_updates: list, sample_counts: list) -> dict:
    """Weighted FedAvg: each hospital's contribution weighted by local dataset size."""
    total = sum(sample_counts)
    global_weights = {}
    for key in local_updates[0]:
        global_weights[key] = sum(
            update[key] * (count / total)
            for update, count in zip(local_updates, sample_counts)
        )
    return global_weights

# Each round: local train → share Δweights → aggregate → broadcast global model
# No patient data is transmitted at any step

Clinical Validation Results

The foundation model is validated on early sepsis detection — one of the highest-stakes clinical prediction tasks, where every hour of earlier identification corresponds to measurably better patient outcomes. The jump from the Random Forest baseline to the federated foundation model represents a clinically significant improvement in discrimination:

The jump from 0.75 (baseline Random Forest) to 0.89+ (federated foundation model) represents a clinically meaningful improvement. At a typical ICU volume of 500 admissions per month, a 14-point AUROC improvement translates to dozens of earlier sepsis identifications per month — giving clinicians a wider intervention window before organ failure becomes irreversible. It also substantially reduces false-positive burden, which is a critical operational metric for clinical alert systems.

The MIMIC-III Tables Used

Six core tables drive the analysis. Working with MIMIC-III's raw CSVs is non-trivial: the data spans a decade of clinical records, uses ICD-9 codes (not ICD-10), and requires careful cohort selection to avoid data leakage from future events into training labels.

TableRows (approx)Use in Pipeline
ADMISSIONS58,976Survival labelling, admission type features, cohort selection
PATIENTS46,520Age, gender — demographic features; deduplication key
ICUSTAYS61,532ICU duration, unit type (MICU, SICU, CCU, CSRU) features
DIAGNOSES_ICD651,047Sepsis cohort selection (ICD-9 codes 995.9x, 785.52), GNN node definitions
CHARTEVENTS~330MVital signs time-series — primary ACSSM input; requires heavy filtering
PROCEDURES_ICD240,095Treatment history for RL state-action pairs and reward labelling

The CHARTEVENTS table deserves special mention: at 330 million rows, it is the largest table by a significant margin and contains every nurse-charted vital sign measurement across the dataset. Working with it requires careful filtering by ITEMID (which maps to specific clinical variables), removal of erroneous values (physiologically impossible measurements exist), and intelligent imputation for periods of missing data — handled by the ACSSM's continuous-dynamics model rather than naive forward-fill.

Ethical Considerations & Deployment Realities

This project is educational and research-grade — and being explicit about what that means matters, particularly for clinical AI work. The gap between a compelling research prototype and a deployable clinical tool is wide, and almost none of it is a technical problem.

For research use only. Real clinical AI deployment requires: (1) prospective validation in the target population (MIMIC-III is from 2001–2012; clinical practice has shifted), (2) IRB/ethics approval, (3) bias auditing across demographic subgroups (age, ethnicity, sex), (4) regulatory approval (MHRA/FDA class IIb medical device pathway for clinical decision support), and (5) continuous monitoring post-deployment with defined drift thresholds. The RL reward functions here are educational simplifications — real clinical RL requires expert clinician involvement in reward design to avoid optimising for metrics that diverge from patient welfare.
