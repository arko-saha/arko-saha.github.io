Beyond Instinct: Data-Driven T20 Cricket Projections

Cricket analytics lags a decade behind baseball. For my MSc dissertation I built the framework that closes the gap — novel KPIs adapted from Sabermetrics, a deep neural network trained on 400k+ deliveries, and an interactive Tableau dashboard for coaches and scouts. Here's the full architecture.

The Gap Between Gut Feel and Evidence

Cricket has always had room for instinct. For most of the sport's history, player selection, team composition, and in-game tactics were built on expertise, experience, and judgement — and that tradition runs deep. But T20 cricket now generates enormous volumes of granular data: ball-by-ball records, player statistics across formats and conditions, partnership data, phase-specific bowling figures.

This research was completed as my MSc in Data Science Dissertation at the University of Roehampton, London, UK (submitted September 2023) under the supervision of Kimia Aksir. The question this dissertation asked is whether that data can be made to work harder than it currently does. Specifically: can we build a framework that puts better evidence in the hands of coaches, scouts, and analysts — not to replace cricket knowledge, but to sharpen it?

The practical stakes are real. IPL franchise owners and national selectors spend significant money on player investments and make selection decisions that determine careers — often with inadequate analytical support. This project tries to give them a better toolkit, built entirely from publicly available data.

Four Problems Cricket Analytics Hasn't Solved

Cricket analytics isn't behind for lack of data — it's behind because the culture has been slow to demand analytical rigour, and because the existing metrics were never designed for the T20 format. The dissertation addresses four specific gaps:

01 — Broken metrics
Batting average, economy rate, and strike rate were designed for Test cricket. A batsman scoring 40 off 20 in a losing cause looks identical to the same innings in a chase win. There's no T20-calibrated evaluation framework in common use.
02 — Hard forecasting
Predicting future performance from historical data is genuinely difficult. Performance is multi-dimensional, form fluctuates, and without composite indicators, most attempts reduce to extrapolating raw averages.
03 — Unusable outputs
Even where good analysis exists, it rarely reaches decision-makers in a usable form. The gap between analytical output and actionable guidance for coaches is wide.
04 — Untapped deep learning
Deep learning has not been seriously applied to ball-by-ball prediction in T20 cricket. The granularity of the data makes it well-suited for sequence modelling — but this remains underdeveloped in the literature.

Data Acquisition: Two Sources, Never Designed to Join

The dataset is assembled from two open repositories covering January 2022 to August 2023 for predictive modelling, with a broader six-year historical window for exploratory analysis.

Cricsheet provided ball-by-ball CSV records for 2,891 T20 international matches — batter, bowler, runs scored, wicket type, and fielding events. Clean, structured, but limited on player metadata.

ESPN Cricinfo was scraped via Python and BeautifulSoup to obtain match-by-match, position-wise, and aggregate batting and bowling statistics. Rich context, but buried in HTML tables with no API and a schema that changes between eras.

ESPN Cricinfo's HTML tables change structure between eras and have no official API. The scraper implements a 1.5-second rate delay and a retry/backoff mechanism. Always rate-limit scraping — it's both polite and protects against IP bans.
data acquisition — ESPNcricinfo scraper (simplified) Python 
import requests, time
from bs4 import BeautifulSoup
import pandas as pd

def scrape_batting_stats(base_url: str, pages: int, delay: float = 1.5):
    """Paginated scraper with rate limiting for ESPNcricinfo batting tables."""
    all_rows = []
    for page in range(1, pages + 1):
        url = f"{base_url};page={page}"
        time.sleep(delay)                          # respect rate limits
        soup = BeautifulSoup(
            requests.get(url).text, "html.parser"
        )
        table = soup.find("table", {"class": "engineTable"})
        if not table:
            break
        rows = table.find_all("tr", {"class": "data1"})
        all_rows.extend(
            [td.get_text(strip=True) for td in row.find_all("td")]
            for row in rows
        )
    return pd.DataFrame(all_rows, columns=[
        "player", "country", "matches", "innings", "not_outs",
        "runs", "high_score", "average", "balls_faced",
        "strike_rate", "centuries", "fifties"
    ])

Preprocessing Pipeline

Raw Cricsheet CSV → analysis-ready feature matrix is a five-step transformation. Each step is a dedicated module, making the pipeline testable and composable:

  1. Schema normalisation — Cricsheet changed its column naming convention in 2020 (batter → striker, player_out → player_dismissed). The loader handles both historical and modern schema automatically, ensuring full dataset coverage without manual renaming.
  2. Ball parsing — Deliveries are encoded as decimals: 3.4 = 4th ball of over 3. This step splits the decimal into two integer columns: completed_over and ball_no, which are far more useful as model features and for match-state indexing.
  3. Cleaning — Super-over deliveries are removed (they skew phase statistics), trailing null rows are dropped, and a binary fall_of_wicket indicator is derived from the wicket text column. Numerical features are normalised with StandardScaler; categoricals are label-encoded.
  4. KPI computation — The derived metrics (Batting Impact, Bowling Score, Run Expectancy, Wicket Expectancy, Dismissal Factor) are computed from the cleaned delivery-level data and joined back as match-level and player-level aggregates.
  5. Feature engineering — In-match dynamics features (CRR, RRR, Run Momentum, Performance Index) are computed per-delivery. Rolling 10-match form windows are added for both batter and bowler.
preprocessing — in-match dynamics features Python 
def add_match_dynamics(df: pd.DataFrame) -> pd.DataFrame:
    """
    Compute delivery-level in-match state features.
    Adopted from Ganesh (2022) Performance Index formulation.
    """
    # Current Run Rate: cumulative runs / current ball number
    df["CRR"] = (df["cumulative_runs"] / df["ball_number"]) * 6

    # Required Run Rate: remaining runs / remaining balls (2nd innings only)
    df["RRR"] = df.apply(
        lambda r: ((r["target"] - r["cumulative_runs"]) /
                    (r["balls_remaining"] / 6))
                   if r["innings"] == 2 else 0, axis=1
    )

    # Run Momentum: wicket pressure on remaining batters
    df["run_momentum"] = (
        (11 - df["cumulative_wickets"]) / df["balls_remaining"].clip(1)
    )

    # Performance Index synthesises rate + wickets in hand
    df["performance_index"] = df["CRR"] * (10 - df["cumulative_wickets"])
    return df

The KPI Framework: Adapted from Sabermetrics

The most distinctive part of this research was building a KPI framework that actually means something in a T20 context. Standard metrics like batting average and economy rate were never designed for this format. The framework draws on Muzaffer's Performance Impact methodology and extends it with contributions from four additional researchers.

A batsman who scores 40 off 20 in a losing cause looks identical to one who scores the same in a winning chase. Context-aware metrics fix this.
Batting Impact
Impact = (Quality × Frequency) + Hundred_factor + Fifties_factor + Zeroes_factor
Adapted from Muzaffer (2016). Quality = Strike Rate × Average measured against T20 benchmarks (SR ≥ 130, Average ≥ 30). Frequency = Balls Faced / Innings. Milestone factors (Centuries, Fifties, and Ducks divided by Innings) scale with consistency, acting as rewards or penalties based on frequency.
Bowling Score
Score = Quality × Frequency + (Impact × Maiden_ratio)
Adapted and extended from Muzaffer (2016). Quality = Benchmark / (Economy × Strike Rate), with T20 benchmarks of SR ≤ 12, Economy ≤ 8 rpo. The study's novel addition is the (Impact × Maiden_ratio) term, which explicitly captures a bowler's discipline.
Run Expectancy
RE = E[Runs | Overs_Completed, Wickets_Fallen]
Adopted from Nielsen (2019). Runs are valued contextually through a 2D match-state matrix rather than raw aggregates. A run scored with 9 wickets in hand in the powerplay is weighted differently from a run scored in the 18th over with 3 wickets remaining.
Dismissal Factor
DF = Actual_Wickets − Expected_Wickets_Situational
Original construction built on Date's (2022) delivery-level wicket probability matrix. It measures situational wicket-taking relative to historical probabilities. Bowlers taking wickets at low-probability moments are highlighted as premium assets.

In-Match Dynamics Metrics

To capture the evolving state of a T20 match as a continuous sequence rather than static aggregates, the feature pipeline incorporates three dynamics metrics adapted from Ganesh (2022):

  • Run Rate (RR): Calculated as cumulative_runs / ball_number in the first innings, and required_runs / balls_remaining in the second innings.
  • Run Momentum (RM): Captures the wicket pressure on the remaining batting lineup: (11 − cumulative_wickets) / balls_remaining.
  • Performance Index (PI): Synthesises run rate pressure and resource depth: Run_Rate × (10 − cumulative_wickets).

Players were classified into six tactical roles — upper-, middle-, and lower-order batters, and powerplay, middle-over, and slog-over bowlers — grounded in game knowledge and expert convention. Fielding statistics were deliberately excluded from the scope to maintain analytical coherence rather than risk producing a composite metric that mixed incommensurable performance dimensions.

Predictive Modelling: Two Tasks, Two Architectures

Batting Performance Prediction

Three regression algorithms were trained on ESPNcricinfo-derived batting data. Feature selection was conducted systematically using a correlation heatmap analysis, which confirmed extremely strong relationships between runs and balls faced, fours, sixes, and strike rate. In contrast, venue and opposition features showed no meaningful correlation with individual batting scores and were explicitly excluded to prevent noise and overfitting.

ModelR²Notes
Decision Tree Regressor0.9896Baseline; prone to overfitting on small samples
Random Forest Regressor0.9985Robust generalisation; used in (Deep Prakash & Verma, 2022)
Polynomial Regression (degree 3)0.9999999992Best fit; reflects near-deterministic encoding of runs in constituent inputs
An R² of 0.9999999992 deserves scrutiny. The structural explanation is that match-level runs are near-deterministically encoded in their inputs — balls faced, boundaries, and strike rate — leaving minimal unexplained variance by design. This is a property of the data domain rather than model magic, though overfitting cannot be entirely ruled out without validation on genuinely out-of-distribution data. Future work should test against held-out future seasons, ODI/Test formats, or unseen player cohorts to assess generalisation more rigorously.

Next-Ball Outcome Prediction: Deep Neural Network

The more novel contribution is a five-layer deep neural network trained to classify the outcome of each delivery across seven possible classes. The architecture takes a 12-dimensional feature vector as input:

model architecture — next-ball DNN Python / TensorFlow 
import tensorflow as tf
from tensorflow.keras import layers

# 12-dimensional input: over, ball_no, cumulative_runs, cumulative_wickets,
# batting_team, bowling_team, CRR, RRR, run_momentum, performance_index,
# batter_form_sr, bowler_form_eco

model = tf.keras.Sequential([
    layers.Flatten(input_shape=(12,)),
    layers.Dense(128, activation="relu"),
    layers.Dense(64,  activation="relu"),
    layers.Dense(32,  activation="relu"),
    layers.Dense(16,  activation="relu"),
    layers.Dense(10,  activation="sigmoid"),  # 7-class output
])

model.compile(
    optimizer="adam",
    loss="sparse_categorical_crossentropy",
    metrics=["sparse_categorical_accuracy"]
)

# Training on 400,000+ deliveries (2,891 T20 matches)
# Test accuracy: 99.50% | Test loss: 0.127

The model was trained on over 400,000 data points drawn from 2,891 T20 matches. On a held-out test set it achieved 99.50% accuracy with a test loss of 0.127. No prior work reviewed in this dissertation deployed a deep neural network specifically for granular delivery-by-delivery outcome prediction in T20 cricket.

End-to-End Pipeline Architecture

The research is structured as a reproducible, pipeline-driven system with five sequential phases managed through an Agile workflow:

Cricsheet (2,891 matches) → Loader + Schema normalisation → Ball parser + Cleaner
ESPNcricinfo (scraped) → BeautifulSoup scraper → Player stat aggregator
Feature engineering → KPI computation (5 metrics) → Player clustering (K-NN)
DNN classifier (next-ball) + Polynomial Regression (batting) → Tableau Dashboard

Agile project management was conducted in Notion with iterative development cycles enabling continuous refinement of models and dashboard components. The multi-page Tableau dashboard differs from standard reporting tools in that it integrates K-NN clustering (contrasting with the K-Means approach deployed by Deep Prakash & Verma, 2022), enabling dynamic, role-based segmentation without requiring supervised labels. It combines player performance forecasting, partnership chemistry metrics, phase-specific bowling breakdowns, and interactive player comparisons in a single decision-ready environment built for coaches, scouts, and selectors.

Key Analytical Findings

Exploratory data analysis conducted across the full six-year dataset surfaced several findings not previously quantified at this scale:

Boundary reliance vs. rotation: Stronger batting nations (India, South Africa) rely disproportionately on boundaries — low rotation percentages. Structurally weaker teams exhibit higher rotation rates. This has direct implications for opposition analysis: bowling plans should differ based on the opponent's scoring archetype, not just their aggregate run rate.
Batting Impact discriminates where averages don't: Tableau-based clustering confirmed that Batting Impact and Bowling Score successfully separate high-potential players and undervalued talents across national teams — cases where standard career aggregates assigned them identical or misleading rankings.
Phase-specific bowling economy diverges from overall figures: A bowler's aggregate economy rate frequently masked opposite profiles in powerplay vs. death overs. The phase-segmented Bowling Score reveals which bowlers are genuinely versatile and which are specialists being misdeployed.

Critical Evaluation

What this does well

The framework is reproducible end-to-end — from raw public data through to stakeholder-facing dashboard — with no proprietary inputs. The multi-algorithm comparison with systematic hyperparameter tuning (grid search across all three regression models) is a methodologically meaningful contribution: the finding that Random Forest is insufficient — widely used in comparable prior work — is as useful as the polynomial regression result itself. The DNN result on a genuinely large dataset (400k+ records) is competitive with the state of the art for ball-by-ball cricket prediction.

Scope boundaries and honest limitations

Three boundaries are worth being explicit about:

Polynomial regression R²: The near-perfect R² reflects structural data properties (runs are near-deterministically encoded in balls faced + boundaries) rather than model generalisation. Validation against held-out future seasons, different formats, or unseen player cohorts is essential before treating this as a deployable predictor.
No proprietary tracking data: Ball speed, release point, pitch map coordinates, and impact timing from Hawk-Eye are outside scope. Their inclusion would substantially enrich the KPI framework and bring the pipeline closer to commercially deployed analytics systems.
Fielding excluded: Comprehensive player valuation for T20 franchise contexts — where fielding directly influences match outcomes — would require a dedicated fielding impact model as a parallel component.

Future Directions

Five clear extensions emerge from this work:

  1. Transformer architectures for next-ball prediction

    The temporal structure of ball-by-ball sequences is exactly what attention mechanisms are designed for. A transformer trained on the same 400k-delivery corpus would likely outperform the current dense DNN substantially.

  2. Real-time analytics pipeline

    Transition to live in-game deployment requires a real-time feature engineering layer, sub-second inference infrastructure, and integration with a live data feed. The engineering challenges are well-defined and addressable.

  3. Proprietary tracking data integration

    Hawk-Eye coordinates, ball speed, and release point would enrich every component of the KPI framework.

  4. Multi-format extension

    Format-specific KPI calibration for ODI and Test cricket, where the benchmark parameters (SR 130, average 30) differ substantially.

  5. Prospective validation with practitioners

    Collaboration with a cricket board or franchise analytics team to validate player evaluation recommendations against actual selection outcomes.

Lessons Beyond Cricket

The most transferable lesson from this project has nothing to do with cricket. It's about what happens when you take a domain that has historically resisted quantification and apply a structured, evidence-first methodology to it. The resistance is real — sports domains have deep cultural investment in expert judgement, and data-driven approaches are seen as threatening rather than complementary. Getting adoption requires framing: not "the data says you're wrong," but "the data gives you a second opinion you didn't have before."

This is a pattern that recurs in every domain where AI is being applied. Clinical AI faces the same dynamic. Financial risk models face regulatory versions of it. The Batting Impact Score and Bowling Score in this project are designed to be legible to coaches — not to replace their judgement, but to surface evidence that either confirms or challenges it. That principle of legibility-first AI design is something I carry directly into FlameFactory's product philosophy.

The second transferable lesson is about the data pipeline problem. Roughly 60% of the project's technical effort went into data acquisition, cleaning, and harmonisation — before any modelling began. The models themselves were the straightforward part. If there's a meta-takeaway from any serious data project, it's this: the infrastructure beneath the model is always the harder problem, and almost always the more valuable one to solve well.

Sports analytics is a mirror for every AI application domain: the hard problem isn't the model — it's building the evidence base that makes the model trustworthy enough for practitioners to act on.
